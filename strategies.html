Thoreau's Approach for Societal and Personal Amendment

Thoreau's Methods for Transformation

To talk about Henry David Thoreau's personal methods for change would be to talk about his philosophy, which would take far too long to cover in depth. His philosophy and how he applied it can be found in his many literary works, such as Walden, and his journals. His methods for societal change also stem from the same root: writing.

Personal Philsophy

Thoreau's personal philosophy revolved around the belief that individuals must live deliberately, valuing their own conscience over the popular preference. He argued for simplicity, self-reliance, finding truth in nature, and never wanting more than you need. While Thoreau never insisted that everyone live exactly as he did, his writings were meant to inspire others to reflect and make change if needed. Ultimately, Thoreau died happy and fulfilled, confident in the life he lived, despite his "hermit-like" reputation.

Major Works by Henry David Thoreau

  • Walden (1854) - A reflection on simple living, self reliance, and nature. Not widely recognized in his lifetime, but highly influential today. Known as a classic American text.
  • Civil Disobedience (1849) - An essay arguing people's moral right to resist unjust laws. Largely ignored during his lifetime, but has influence countless social justice movements and leaders today. Seen as a classic American text.
  • A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers (1849) - A narrative of Thoreau's river journey with his brother, incorporating travel writing and philosophical thought. It had limited impact during his lifetime.
  • Walking (1862) - An essay about nature's spiritual significance as well as the importance of preserving wilderness. Not well known in his lifetime, but is today seen as foundational for environmental ethics.
  • Life Without Principle (1863) - An essay which critiqued materialism and urged people to pursue meaningful work. Not very influential in his time, but studied today in discussions of capitalism and ethics.
  • The Journal of Henry David Thoreau (1837-1861) - Thoreau's journal on observations of nature, society, philosophy. Published after his death, it is seen as an important document in American history and philosphy.
